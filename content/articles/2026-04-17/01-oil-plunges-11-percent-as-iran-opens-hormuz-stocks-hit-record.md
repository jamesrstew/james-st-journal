Oil prices tumbled more than 10 percent and U.S. stocks rallied toward another record Friday after Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi declared the Strait of Hormuz "completely open" for the duration of a 10-day Israel-Lebanon ceasefire that took effect late Thursday. Benchmark U.S. crude fell 10.8 percent to $81.28 a barrel and Brent dropped 10.3 percent to $89.13, according to PBS NewsHour, while the S&P 500 climbed 0.8 percent and the Dow Jones Industrial Average added 678 points in early trading.

The reopening marks the clearest break yet in a seven-week war that had choked off a waterway carrying roughly a fifth of the world's oil and liquefied natural gas. It also sets up the sharpest test to date of whether the Trump administration's pressure campaign can convert a battlefield pause into a durable deal before the ceasefire clock runs out — even as President Trump said the U.S. naval blockade of Iranian ports will remain in force until Tehran agrees to terms.

## What shifted

Araghchi announced the opening in a post on X on Friday. "In line with the ceasefire in Lebanon, the passage for all commercial vessels through Strait of Hormuz is declared completely open for the remaining period of ceasefire," he wrote, adding that ships would follow a coordinated route previously announced by the Ports and Maritime Organisation of Iran. A senior Iranian military official told state media that only nonmilitary vessels would be allowed to transit, and only with permission from the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps Navy.

Trump confirmed the opening in a social media post but drew a line under the U.S. cordon. The strait was "completely open and ready for business and full passage," he wrote, though the U.S. blockade of Iran would "remain in full force" until Iran reaches a deal with Washington to end the war. The ceasefire followed a 21-hour round of U.S.-Iran talks in Islamabad that broke up without agreement on April 12, after which U.S. warships sealed Iran's ports.

## On the street

The market response was the sharpest single-day repricing since the U.S. Navy cordon took effect on April 13. Stocks have now climbed more than 11 percent since hitting a bottom in late March, and are running toward the finish of a third straight week of big gains — their longest such streak since Halloween, PBS reported. In the bond market, the yield on the 10-year Treasury slipped to 4.24 percent from 4.32 percent late Thursday as falling oil took pressure off inflation expectations.

European equities tracked the move. France's CAC 40 jumped 2 percent and Germany's DAX gained 2.2 percent. Asian indexes, which closed before Araghchi's announcement, were weaker, with Japan's Nikkei 225 down 1.8 percent and Hong Kong's Hang Seng off 0.9 percent. Crude remains well above its roughly $70 level from before the war, a sign that traders are pricing in residual risk rather than a full resolution.

## The blockade holds

The Pentagon is not standing down. The U.S. military has sent the aircraft carrier USS George H.W. Bush and escorting warships to the Middle East, adding about 6,000 troops to a force that already includes 10,000 personnel, a dozen warships and more than 100 aircraft enforcing the cordon, according to U.S. Central Command. "We can do this all day," Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth said at a Pentagon briefing Thursday. No ships have broken through, Central Command said, and 10 merchant vessels have complied with orders to turn around and reenter Iranian ports on the Gulf of Oman.

At the United Nations, officials are racing to ease a secondary shock. Jorge Moreira da Silva, who runs the U.N. Office for Project Services, told NPR he is pitching a short-term mechanism to clear cargo ships carrying fertilizer and related raw materials through the strait, warning that roughly 45 million people could be pushed into hunger in the coming weeks without one. "One-third of all fertilizers in the world are coming from the Persian Gulf," Moreira da Silva said, naming Sudan, Somalia, Mozambique, Kenya and Sri Lanka among the most exposed importers.

## Counterpoint

The posture from Washington's critics is that the blockade itself is the problem. China's foreign ministry has called the U.S. cordon "dangerous and irresponsible," and Iran has warned the measure is an act of war that could unravel the ceasefire. Administration officials beyond Trump and Hegseth had not offered a detailed public response to the reopening by press time, and no Republican congressional leader had spoken on the record about how long the naval cordon should remain in place if Tehran continues to allow commercial traffic.

## What comes next

The ceasefire runs 10 days. A second round of U.S.-Iran talks is expected before it expires, after Pakistan's army chief, Field Marshal Asim Munir, met Araghchi in Tehran on Wednesday to lay the groundwork. Shipowners will watch whether the IRGC Navy's permitting regime moves tankers in practice, and whether Washington's blockade of Iranian ports begins to interdict Chinese-flagged vessels carrying Iranian crude — a step analysts have warned could drag Beijing directly into the standoff. For now, the market is betting that the guns stay quiet long enough for the oil to move.
