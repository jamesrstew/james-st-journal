Pope Leo XIV drew about 120,000 worshippers to an open-air Mass in Douala, Cameroon, on Friday, using one of the biggest stages of his Africa tour to press a peace message that has put him on a collision course with the Trump administration. A day earlier, in the separatist-riven city of Bamenda, the pontiff condemned a world "ravaged by a handful of tyrants" who spend billions on war. President Trump over the weekend called the pope "weak on crime and soft on foreign policy," and Leo has responded that he has "no fear of the Trump administration," even as fresh polling points to softening Catholic support for the president after his attack on the Vatican.

The standoff is the most public breach between a sitting U.S. president and a pope in modern memory, and it is unfolding as Washington presses Tehran toward a renewed ceasefire before an April 22 deadline. Leo, the first American to lead the Catholic Church, has made opposition to the U.S. and Israeli-led war in Iran the defining theme of his young pontificate. Trump, facing 2026 midterm elections with gasoline up and inflation at 3.3 percent, has cast the pope as a political adversary rather than a moral interlocutor.

## Cameroon stage

Al Jazeera reported that the faithful began filing into Douala's Japorma stadium a day early, sleeping on concrete through the mosquitoes and cold to secure a view. In his homily, delivered in English and French, Leo urged the city's youth: "Reject every form of abuse or violence, which deceives by promising easy gains but hardens the heart." The stop followed Thursday's visit to Bamenda, the epicenter of a nearly decade-long Anglophone insurgency that the United Nations says has killed more than 6,500 people and displaced over 650,000 since 2017.

## Rebuke from the pulpit

At St. Joseph's Cathedral in Bamenda, Leo took direct aim at leaders who invoke Scripture to justify force. "Woe to those who manipulate religion and the very name of God for their own military, economic and political gain, dragging that which is sacred into darkness and filth," he said, according to NPR. PBS NewsHour noted the line landed against the backdrop of Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth's frequent use of biblical language to defend the Iran campaign, and of Vice President JD Vance's warning that the pope should "be careful" when speaking on theology. NPR reported that Vance, a Catholic convert, also told Leo to "stay out of politics" and focus on "matters of morality."

## White House response

Trump's weekend broadside extended beyond policy. NPR reported that the president briefly shared an AI-generated image of himself depicted as Christ before deleting it, while urging Leo to stick to religious matters. Al Jazeera reported that the pope's criticism of the Iran war prompted Trump to call the first U.S.-born pontiff "weak" and "terrible for foreign policy," and that Leo in turn said he has "no fear of the Trump administration." A new poll this week showed the president's support slipping among Catholic voters after the exchange.

## Conservative counterpoint

Right-leaning commentators argue the pope is the one who has stepped out of his lane. Writing in RealClearPolitics, J. Peder Zane contended that Leo's "ramped-up criticism of the Iran war" came suspiciously soon after a Vatican audience with David Axelrod, the longtime Democratic strategist, and called the timing a "nakedly political act" aimed at the midterms. Zane also faulted the pope for what he called selective morality, citing Leo's muted response to Iran's crackdown on protesters in January, when the Vatican urged that "dialogue and peace may be patiently nurtured," and argued that partisan engagement erodes the moral authority the church still needs to command.

## What comes next

Leo travels from Cameroon to Angola and Equatorial Guinea next, closing a four-country Africa tour that began in Algeria. U.S. and Iranian negotiators are due back at the table in Islamabad before the April 22 ceasefire expires, with Hegseth warning that American forces remain ready to resume strikes. Whether the pope's Douala homily reaches the negotiating room is an open question. For now, two American leaders with very different congregations are speaking past each other on the same war.
