Health Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. appeared before the House Appropriations Committee on Thursday for a second day of testimony on his department's agenda and a proposed budget cut of more than 12 percent, as President Trump nominated Erica Schwartz, a former deputy surgeon general, to run the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. The two developments placed the administration's overhaul of federal health policy squarely in front of Congress on the same afternoon.

The session and the nomination together frame a fight over vaccine policy, leadership turnover at the CDC and the size of the federal public health budget. Kennedy is working through a sprint of seven budget hearings across congressional committees and subcommittees over the course of a week, while Schwartz would take charge of an agency that has cycled through acting directors since the firing of Susan Monarez last year.

## Budget defense

Kennedy used the hearings to defend the proposed reduction and to promote the administration's work on dietary guidelines and what he described as efforts to crack down on waste, fraud and abuse. Republicans on the panel praised him as a "breath of fresh air," according to PBS NewsHour. Democrats pressed him on cuts to individual programs and on changes to CDC vaccine messaging, which they said had contributed to measles outbreaks.

In one exchange, Rep. Linda Sanchez, Democrat of California, asked Kennedy whether the president had approved ending the CDC's pro-vaccine public messaging campaign. Kennedy did not answer directly, saying he first wanted to address what he called misstatements. Sanchez also drew an acknowledgement from Kennedy that a 6-year-old who died of measles in West Texas last year could have been helped by vaccination. "It's possible, certainly," he said.

## CDC pick

Schwartz, whose nomination requires Senate confirmation, served as deputy surgeon general during the first Trump administration and played a role in the federal Covid-19 response. She spent more than 20 years in uniform, rising to rear admiral and chief medical officer of the Coast Guard. Trump described her in a social media post as "incredibly talented" and said, "She is a STAR!"

The White House also named Sean Slovenski as CDC deputy director and chief operating officer, and Dr. Jennifer Shuford, the Texas health commissioner who led that state's response to last year's measles outbreak, as deputy director and chief medical officer. Dr. Jay Bhattacharya, the National Institutes of Health director, had been running the CDC on an acting basis after the 210-day limit under the Vacancies Act expired last month.

## Missing voices

Wire coverage from PBS NewsHour and CNBC focused on the hearings and the nomination without capturing the full range of partisan reaction. Committee Democrats had not released a formal statement on Schwartz by press time, no Senate Republican had publicly committed to a confirmation timetable, and mainstream vaccine-policy advocates had not provided comment on the nomination. Aaron Siri, a lawyer allied with Kennedy on vaccine litigation, criticized the pick, saying Schwartz "lacks the basic ethics and morals to lead the CDC," according to PBS NewsHour. Schwartz could not be reached for comment.

Kennedy is scheduled to appear before the House Education and Workforce Committee on Friday at 9 a.m. Eastern, continuing the week of budget testimony as the Senate prepares to schedule a confirmation hearing for Schwartz.
