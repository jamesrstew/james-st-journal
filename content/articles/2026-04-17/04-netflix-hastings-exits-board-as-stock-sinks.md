Reed Hastings, the co-founder who built Netflix into the world's largest streaming service, will step down from its board in June when his term expires, the company disclosed Thursday alongside a first-quarter earnings report that sent the stock sliding about 10% in extended trading. Netflix beat Wall Street on revenue at $12.25 billion, up 16% from a year earlier, but it held its full-year revenue forecast of $50.7 billion to $51.7 billion even after walking away in February from its proposed $72 billion purchase of Warner Bros. Discovery's studio and streaming assets.

Hastings' departure, combined with unchanged guidance and the collapse of Netflix's largest-ever deal attempt, puts the streamer's next chapter into sharp relief. Co-CEO Ted Sarandos used the earnings call to argue that the aborted WBD bid proved Netflix's deal muscle rather than exposed a strategy gap, but the company now faces a consolidating market without the founder who defined its builder-not-buyer identity.

## Guidance holds, stock slides

Netflix reported net income of $5.28 billion, or $1.23 per share, helped by a $2.8 billion breakup fee collected after Paramount Skydance topped its WBD offer. Reported earnings were not comparable with the 76-cent analyst consensus because of the fee. The company said second-quarter revenue will rise 13%, and Chief Financial Officer Spencer Neumann told investors some WBD-related costs once slated for 2027 would be pulled into 2026.

"The bigger surprise this quarter was the unchanged full-year margin guidance despite walking away from the Warner Bros. deal and related M&A costs," analyst Robert Fishman of MoffettNathanson wrote in a Friday research note.

## M&A muscle, or a one-off

Sarandos told analysts the WBD process taught Netflix "so much about deal execution, about early integration" and that "we really built our M&A muscle." He also pushed back on the idea that Hastings' exit was tied to the failed transaction, saying the co-founder was "a big champion for that deal" and that the board vote to pursue it had been unanimous.

The pivot, however brief, has reset expectations. Netflix's shares fell 15% between the December announcement of the WBD deal and the day it collapsed, then rose roughly 26% afterward, a round trip that leaves investors weighing whether the company will hunt again.

## Core business, core pitch

Management steered the call back to familiar ground: 325 million global paid subscribers as of January, recent price increases the company said were landing as expected, and a reiterated target of $3 billion in advertising revenue in 2026, double last year's level. Co-CEO Greg Peters said the latest price rollout is still under way and tracking with prior increases.

Only CNBC covered Thursday's report in today's dossier, and Netflix's management had not offered public comment beyond the earnings call and shareholder letter. No analyst short-seller or competitor response appeared in the reporting available by press time, and an expected Paramount-WBD combination, flagged by Forrester's Mike Proulx as a landscape-shifting risk, remains contingent on regulatory approval.

Hastings formally exits in June, when his board term ends. Netflix's second-quarter results will be the first test of whether the reiterated guidance holds, with the promised doubling of ad revenue and a potential expansion of its NFL relationship still to be proved out.
