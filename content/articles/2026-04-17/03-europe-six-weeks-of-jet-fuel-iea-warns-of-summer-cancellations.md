Europe has roughly six weeks of jet fuel left, International Energy Agency Director Fatih Birol told the Associated Press on Thursday, warning that airlines across the continent face summer flight cancellations if crude oil does not start moving again through the Strait of Hormuz. Carriers from Amsterdam to Atlanta are already raising fares, adding surcharges and lifting checked-bag fees to offset jet fuel prices that have roughly doubled since the Iran war began.

The squeeze on refined products is the sharpest economic shock yet from a war that has shut the strait to tanker traffic. Birol called it the largest energy crisis the world economy has faced. The last jet fuel shipment to clear the strait reached Europe last week after loading on Feb. 28, before the fighting started.

## The supply math

Jet fuel, a kerosene-based product refined from crude, accounts for about 30 percent of airline operating expenses, according to the International Air Transport Association. Roughly 40 percent of Europe's jet fuel imports normally come through the Strait of Hormuz, Argus Media's Amaar Khan told the AP, and none has passed since the war broke out. Several European countries are now operating on fewer than 20 days of coverage, the IEA said in a report this week, down from a floor of 29 days held since 2020. If stockpiles slip below 23 days, the agency warned, shortages could surface at airports and force cancellations.

## Carriers cut flights

Dutch carrier KLM said Thursday it will cancel 160 flights next month, about 1 percent of its European schedule, citing rising kerosene costs and flights that are "no longer financially viable to operate." Lufthansa said the same day it will immediately wind down feeder airline CityLine and retire 27 older, less fuel-efficient aircraft. EasyJet guided to a pretax loss of 540 million to 560 million pounds, or roughly $731 million to $758 million, for the first half of its 2026 fiscal year, though Chief Executive Kenton Jarvis said demand remains strong.

## U.S. carriers pass it on

Delta Air Lines told investors that higher fuel prices will cost an additional $2 billion this quarter. Chief Executive Ed Bastian said the airline is cutting unprofitable routes and recapturing costs by raising ticket prices, and he said customers are still buying. Delta, United, American, Southwest and JetBlue have all raised checked-bag fees in recent weeks. U.S. airlines abandoned the fuel-hedging programs that blunted past spikes, leaving them exposed to the full run-up.

## Asia is worse off

The top three global jet fuel exporters — China, South Korea and Kuwait — are each sidelined. China has banned jet fuel exports and South Korea has cut production because neither can secure enough crude, while Kuwait can still refine but cannot ship. "This is an Asian crisis," Kpler analyst George Shaw told NPR. "They're in a worse position than anyone else."

## Industry view is thin

Airline officials have so far reacted with caution. KLM and easyJet told the AP they are not currently experiencing shortages, and Delta said it does not expect any near-term hit to operations, pointing to the Philadelphia refinery it bought in 2012. Some analysts cited by NPR are skeptical that shortages will set in as quickly as airport operators fear. OPEC producers and the main airline trade groups did not feature in today's wire reporting, and the producer-side case for when supply resumes is thin in the record.

The near-term test is the end of April, when the Airports Council International Europe has told the European Commission that "significant and stable" passage through the strait must resume or a systemic shortage will set in across the European Union. Even if tankers start moving soon, Shaw said, it will take weeks for the first barrels to reach European and Asian refineries — meaning the summer travel season will begin with the squeeze still on.
