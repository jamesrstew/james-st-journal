Europe has roughly six weeks of jet fuel left before stocks reach a tipping point, International Energy Agency Executive Director Fatih Birol told the Associated Press on Thursday, warning that flight cancellations could begin by June if the Strait of Hormuz remains closed. The IEA said the global economy faces its "largest energy crisis," and that Europe has historically relied on the Middle East for about 75 percent of its jet fuel imports.

If Europe cannot replace more than half of its Middle Eastern supplies, the IEA said, physical shortages may emerge at select airports, triggering cancellations and "demand destruction" at the start of the summer travel season. Jet fuel typically makes up 20 percent to 40 percent of airline operating costs, and carriers are already passing the bill to passengers through higher fares, fuel surcharges and baggage fees.

## Prices double, fees climb

The benchmark European jet fuel price hit an all-time high of $1,838 per tonne at the start of April, more than double the $831 level before the war in Iran began, according to the BBC. Delta Air Lines, United Airlines, American Airlines, Southwest Airlines and JetBlue have all raised checked-baggage fees in recent weeks. Hong Kong's Cathay Pacific lifted fuel surcharges about 34 percent across all routes, and Air India added up to $280 in fees on some flights this month.

## Carriers cut routes

Dutch carrier KLM said Thursday it will cancel 160 European flights next month, about 1 percent of its regional schedule, citing "rising kerosene costs." EasyJet warned of a pretax loss of 540 million to 560 million pounds, or about $731 million to $758 million, for the first half of fiscal 2026, after absorbing 25 million pounds in additional March fuel costs. Lufthansa said it will shut its CityLine feeder unit and ground 27 older planes. United CEO Scott Kirby told staff sustained high prices could add $11 billion to annual costs, more than twice the carrier's best-ever profit.

## Hormuz at the center

The Strait of Hormuz has been effectively closed by Iran for more than six weeks, and Airports Council International Europe has warned the European Commission that a "systemic jet fuel shortage is set to become a reality for the EU" if passage does not resume by the end of April. The top three global jet fuel exporters — China, South Korea and Kuwait — are all knocked offline, according to Kpler analyst George Shaw, either because they cannot source crude or cannot ship finished product.

## The reopening caveat

Even if the strait reopens in the near future, a restart would not quickly undo the squeeze on European carriers. Argus Media's Amaar Khan said replacement cargoes would take "five to six weeks" to arrive even if supply resumes, and Rystad Energy estimates Middle East oil and gas facilities have suffered as much as $50 billion in war damage — delays and destruction that would still flow through to the fares, surcharges and cancellations already hitting passengers this spring.

The IEA's next monthly update lands in mid-May, just as European carriers publish summer schedules. Delta CEO Ed Bastian told investors the airline is cutting unprofitable routes and "recapturing" fuel costs through fares. Travelers, Cornell operations professor Christopher Anderson said, face "later booking patterns, more schedule volatility and fewer low-fare options."
