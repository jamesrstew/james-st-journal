The Federal Reserve held its benchmark interest rate steady on Wednesday but moved meaningfully closer to a summer rate cut, with Chair Jerome Powell telling reporters the central bank was "closer to the moment" of easing than at any point since it began its inflation fight.

Officials left the federal funds target in a range of 4.25 percent to 4.50 percent, unchanged since January, and the committee's statement swapped its long-standing "patient" posture for language flagging that the risks to employment had "come into better balance" with the risks to prices.

Markets took the shift as a green light. The two-year Treasury yield fell nine basis points to 3.78 percent, its lowest level of the year, and fed funds futures now imply roughly a 70 percent probability of a quarter-point cut at the June meeting, up from 45 percent before the decision.

The S&P 500 closed up 1.1 percent at a record. The dollar weakened against every major currency.

## What shifted

Three data points, officials said, had reset the picture. Payroll growth had slowed to an average of 118,000 jobs in the three months through March, down from 214,000 at year-end. The services component of the consumer price index rose 0.2 percent in March, the smallest increase in more than a year. And the Atlanta Fed's wage tracker fell to 3.9 percent, dipping below 4 percent for the first time since 2021.

"None of these is decisive on its own," Powell said. "Taken together, they describe an economy that is returning to balance without having required a recession to get there."

He declined to pre-commit to June, warning that "a single hot print" could still push the committee to wait. But he offered an unusually direct sketch of the case for cutting, citing the risk of holding rates restrictive for longer than the economy requires.

## The dovish dissent

Governor Lisa Cook dissented in favor of an immediate quarter-point cut, writing in a brief statement that labor-market softening had already moved further than the committee's own projections from December had anticipated, and that waiting another six weeks carried asymmetric risk.

It was the first dissent of Powell's second term and the first formal call for easing from a sitting governor since 2019.

## On the Street

Bond desks described the afternoon as a relief rally more than a celebration. "This isn't 2019-style insurance cuts being priced," said one rates strategist at a primary dealer. "It's the committee conceding that the curve was right all along."

Equity sentiment was more exuberant. Small caps, which are more sensitive to financing costs, outperformed, with the Russell 2000 up 2.4 percent. Regional bank shares rallied on expectations that the deposit-cost squeeze will ease.

Not all the reaction was positive. The KBW Bank Index gave up part of its gains after Powell, in response to a question, said the committee saw no reason to slow the pace of balance-sheet runoff, which has continued to drain reserves from the banking system.

## The political weather

The decision landed into a charged political environment, with the administration openly pressing for faster cuts and Senate Banking Committee members in both parties scheduled to question Powell in testimony later this month.

Powell declined to be drawn on politics. Asked whether public pressure had entered the committee's deliberations, he gave a one-word answer: "No."

The next meeting is June 16–17. The committee will update its summary of economic projections at that meeting, giving markets their first look at a revised dot plot since the March "higher-for-longer" forecast.
