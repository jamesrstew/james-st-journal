The Senate Banking Committee voted 18 to 5 on Wednesday to advance legislation that would, for the first time, create a federal framework for issuing and holding dollar-pegged stablecoins, clearing the most significant procedural hurdle the industry has faced in Washington.

The bill, a compromise between Chair Tim Scott (R., S.C.) and Senator Kirsten Gillibrand (D., N.Y.), would allow banks, credit unions, and approved non-bank issuers to issue stablecoins under either federal or state supervision, provided the coins are fully backed by cash or short-term Treasuries and redeemable at par on demand.

Four Democrats and all committee Republicans voted yes. Senator Elizabeth Warren (D., Mass.), one of the bill's most persistent critics, voted no and warned on the committee floor that the measure would "bolt a wing onto the dollar that the Fed does not control."

## What's in it

Three provisions carry most of the weight. First, a reserve mandate: issuers would hold 100 percent backing in cash, overnight repos, or Treasury bills of 93 days or fewer, with monthly public attestations by an independent auditor. Second, a bankruptcy carve-out: in the event of an issuer failure, holders' claims on the reserves would be senior to all other creditors, resolving a legal gray area that has spooked institutional adopters. Third, a preemption clause limiting state authority to charter new issuers after 2028.

The preemption clause, more than reserves, is what moved the vote. Industry lobbyists had spent two years arguing that the current patchwork — in which New York's BitLicense and a handful of state trust charters compete with a stalled federal regime — had left U.S. issuers at a disadvantage to offshore rivals.

## Why now

The committee's vote followed six months of pressure from an unlikely coalition: payment processors, which want lower-cost dollar rails for cross-border transfers; regional banks, which see stablecoin issuance as a defense against disintermediation; and community Democrats, who have grown warmer to the idea after reports that a growing share of remittances to Latin America now move via dollar-pegged tokens.

Treasury Secretary Janet Yellen, who had opposed earlier versions of the bill, sent a letter to the committee last week indicating the department would not object to the compromise, a shift industry lawyers attribute to the bill's strict reserve requirements.

## The floor ahead

The bill now heads to the full Senate, where Majority Leader Chuck Schumer has not committed to a timeline. Senate rules on cryptocurrency-adjacent legislation have become more fraught since last summer's memecoin scandal, and Schumer's office declined to say whether floor time would be found before the August recess.

House prospects are less clear. Financial Services Chair Patrick McHenry has said he prefers his own framework, which treats stablecoins as a form of regulated narrow banking, and has given no public signal on whether he would accept the Senate bill as written.

Industry reaction was cautiously positive. A trade group representing the largest U.S. issuers called the vote "the most constructive step Washington has taken on stablecoins since 2022." A second group, representing smaller issuers, warned that the preemption clause would consolidate issuance among the three or four largest incumbents.

The five no votes came from three Democrats and two Republicans. None cited the same reason.
