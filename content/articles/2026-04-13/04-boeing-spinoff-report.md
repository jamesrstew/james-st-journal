Boeing is studying a plan to separate its defense, space, and security business into a standalone public company, a move that would unwind more than two decades of integration and hand its incoming chief executive a cleaner commercial-aviation franchise to rehabilitate, according to three people familiar with the deliberations.

The review, which has been under way since the board meeting in February, has advanced to the point that bankers have been asked to sketch capital-structure scenarios, the people said. No decision has been made and the board is not expected to act before its June strategy off-site, one of them added.

Boeing shares rose 6.4 percent on the report, their best day since January.

A Boeing spokesman declined to comment, saying the company does not respond to speculation about corporate strategy.

## Why now

Pressure for a split has been building since last year's guilty plea in the 737 MAX prosecution and the subsequent management change. Two activist investors with combined stakes estimated at more than 4 percent have privately pressed directors on the case for separation, arguing that the market values the defense unit at a multiple implied by the conglomerate's troubled commercial arm rather than the peer-group multiple enjoyed by pure-play defense primes.

One sell-side analysis circulated last month pegged the sum-of-the-parts at roughly $220 billion, against an enterprise value near $170 billion at current prices.

Boeing's defense unit posted operating income of $1.4 billion in the fourth quarter, a margin profile closer to Lockheed Martin and RTX than to the loss-making commercial operation. Spun off, the people said, it would become one of the five largest U.S. defense contractors by revenue and would likely be added to major defense indices within a quarter.

## The hard parts

Three obstacles stand between the idea and execution, the people said.

Shared engineering talent. Thousands of engineers move between defense and commercial programs, sometimes on the same platform; untangling labor pools without impairing either business is the problem bankers have been asked to model.

Pension liabilities. The company's defined-benefit obligations, a legacy of the McDonnell Douglas merger, are not cleanly attributable to one segment, and allocating them between the two successor companies would require tough negotiation over who absorbs the hedging cost.

Customer concentration. Several commercial-aviation customers hold framework agreements that pool pricing and service terms with defense buys, particularly among foreign governments that operate both fleets. Those agreements would need to be renegotiated or novated.

Any one of those issues, the people said, could delay the transaction by a year or kill it.

## The CEO question

The review is happening in parallel with a CEO search that entered final interviews this month. A decision on separation, one of the people said, will likely follow the hire rather than precede it, because the board wants the incoming executive to own the decision on the shape of the business they are inheriting.

Two of the three reported finalists run pure-play defense companies; the third is an outside-industry turnaround specialist with commercial-manufacturing experience. Industry observers have read the composition of the shortlist itself as a signal that separation is on the table.

An announcement, if it comes, is unlikely before the second half. Any transaction would take a further 12 to 18 months to close.
