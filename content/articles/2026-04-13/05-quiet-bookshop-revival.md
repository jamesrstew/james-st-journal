The independent bookshop, written off as a casualty of e-commerce sometime around 2011, has spent the past three years quietly doing the opposite of dying. The American Booksellers Association counted 2,847 member storefronts at the end of last year, a net addition of 214 locations over the prior 12 months and the third consecutive year of growth.

The openings are not nostalgic. They are something stranger.

The stores opening now are, on average, smaller than the ones they replace — median footprint 1,100 square feet, down from 2,400 a decade ago. Their inventories lean hard on frontlist and staff picks rather than deep backlist, a function of both space and a different customer. Nearly half of them include a café, a wine bar, or both.

"We are not selling books," the owner of one year-old shop in Portland said. "We are selling the feeling of having a reason to leave the house."

## The demographic

The customer making these shops viable is, against every prediction, younger than the customer who kept the old ones alive. Industry data pegs 18-to-34-year-olds at 38 percent of indie-bookshop revenue, up from 22 percent in 2018. The same cohort accounts for the lion's share of growth in physical-format fiction sales, especially in genres — romance, fantasy, speculative fiction — that had been written off as fully digital.

The working theory among booksellers is not that this generation has rediscovered literature. It is that they are starved for social contexts that do not involve a screen, and that a small shop with chairs and coffee and a reason to linger is one of the few commercial settings that delivers that without requiring either a drink order or a reservation.

## The economics

The economics of a bookshop are, in absolute terms, still terrible. Margins on new books sit around 30 to 35 percent before rent, labor, and the cost of returns. Most of the shops opening now survive on a blended model: used books, which carry far better margins, make up 25 to 40 percent of inventory. Events — author readings, book clubs, writing workshops — drive foot traffic and deliver a second margin through food and drink. A growing share of revenue comes from non-book items: notebooks, tote bags, greeting cards, puzzles.

Bookshop.org, the online cooperative founded in 2020 to give independents a fighting chance against Amazon, passed $400 million in cumulative sales to participating stores last year. It is not enough to make any single store comfortable, but it is enough, margin by margin, to keep the lights on.

## The thing nobody predicted

What is strange about the revival is how consistent the formula is across very different cities. The shops opening in Boise look and operate very like the shops opening in Brooklyn: tight selections, events two or three nights a week, a drink menu, a point of view.

One longtime industry consultant put it this way last month: "The chain bookstore died because it was trying to be everything. The independent is coming back because it has figured out how to be one specific thing very clearly, and to be honest about what it is not."

The trend is small. Two hundred stores is not a revolution. But for a category that was supposed to be extinct, it is a remarkable thing to be doing at all.
